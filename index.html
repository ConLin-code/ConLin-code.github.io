My Story

The Beginning

As a young boy, I was always known as “the tech kid.” I would spend hours each day on my iPod—watching shows like Avatar: The Last Airbender, playing games like Subway Surfers and Bloons Tower Defense, and just exploring content. Concerned about my screen time, my parents began enforcing strict time limits. But in a surprising twist, that simple restriction sparked an even deeper curiosity—one that led me to explore technology. Why? To find ways to give myself more time to play games. And how? By searching for any glitch or loophole in the system I could find. This is what first ignited my fascination with tech.

A New Interest

As more and more people started calling me “the tech kid,” I became even more curious about engineering and computer science. I started learning the basics—how code works, how sensors function, and how the technology I enjoyed actually operated. It was fascinating. Over time, though, another field began to catch my eye: finance. Just like technology had once captured my interest, finance became the new focus of my curiosity. I began researching why stock prices rise and fall, what a stock actually represents, and what factors influence the market. My fascination grew so much that my mom opened a custodial account for me, in which she deposited the money I received from birthdays and holidays. I’d ask her to place trades on my behalf and would spend hours simply watching the charts. I was hooked.

The struggles throughout Freshman year...

In middle school, I was always drawn to math, especially because it came naturally to me. I was fascinated by proofs and logical reasoning, and I looked forward to math class every day. But in 8th grade, everything changed. My school changed their teaching from showing proofs in which we could reason to conclusions, to mere memorization for the sake of test scores. My passion quickly faded, and my grade dropped just below the threshold needed for high school credit. Because of that, I had to retake Geometry as a freshman at Calvary Christian Academy. The transition to this school wasn’t easy for me. It was over 45 minutes from my home, compared to the school I used to walk to, and since both my parents worked my brother and I took the bus every day, Monday through Friday. I used the time to read books on finance, health, and machine learning while making some great friends along the way. I read books that included The Intelligent Investor, Outlive, and even Superintelligence. Whether I was using my phone flashlight at 6:30 a.m. in the dark or sweating through a hot afternoon in Florida's blazing sun with no AC on the bus, I was still once again hooked. Ironically, though, when I got to school, I found most subjects simply boring. Even my once loved subject math felt dull. Geometry felt repetitive, as it was my second time taking this course, and I simply wasn't engaged. Because of this, throughout my classes I started reading books on the subjects that actually interested me instead of paying attention to what my teacher had to say. It showed in my grades, and I ended up wasting an opportunity that my parents had worked so hard to provide.

Finding light in darkness

As freshman year came to an end, my anxious mother insisted on having a serious talk with me. Concerned about my grades, she said, “Why are you struggling in classes? I pay too much for you to go to this school and learn nothing.” Hearing this simple phrase changed my mindset. I asked her how much she and my father paid, and she told me, “$18,000 a year.” I was shocked—I had no idea my parents were spending this much, sometimes even taking out loans, just to give me an education. And what was I doing with that opportunity? Nothing. Right then and there, I knew something had to change. I started thinking through my options, with thoughts flooding my mind: Why didn’t I find school appealing? What could make it more interesting? Was it possible to learn what truly fascinated me while still keeping up with schoolwork? Then an intriguing question struck me—what if I could create the environment I wanted within the school itself? I decided to take action. My school didn’t have an active finance club, so I founded one. I started attending any club that caught my eye. And I sought out courses that sparked my curiosity, even though my poor grades prevented me from joining advanced classes. With the help of some teachers I enrolled in electives that aligned with my interests: engineering, computer science, and personal finance. And for the rest of the year, I made the most of what was available: I joined different clubs, took some of the toughest classes I could, and pushed myself to learn and grow in school. But outside of school, I was still learning. I was continuing to read books on the bus ride to and from school, and I would still think of curious things with my brother. I finally felt like I was making the most of both my school life and my personal curiosity.

But Nobody’s Done This Before

My sophomore year had just ended, and my brother was preparing to leave for college. I met with my high school counselor to plan my future classes, but I quickly learned that many of the courses I wanted were out of reach. The school’s normal progression meant I couldn’t jump straight from regular classes to AP. Seniors had priority for classes like College Algebra and Statistics, and some teachers had left, leaving only online options for the courses I wanted. Frustrated but hopeful, I decided to take a break and enjoy summer vacation. During the summer, I traveled with my family and eventually found myself back home, searching for new ways to learn. That’s when I discovered online education platforms like MIT OpenCourseWare, Stanford Online, Y Combinator, Michael Saylor Academy, Modern States, and Coursera. The idea of learning from top universities for free, at my own pace, was incredible. Around the same time, my brother and I began researching successful individuals—Steve Jobs, Elon Musk, Mark Zuckerberg, Jeff Bezos—and started listening to their interviews and podcasts. One interview with Steve Jobs stood out. He spoke about Alexander the Great and his tutor, Aristotle, and how Jobs dreamed of capturing Aristotle’s worldview in a computer. This inspired me to imagine a new way of learning: using free online lectures, taking notes, completing assignments, and having AI as a personal tutor. I tried it for a few weeks and loved it so much that it made me question why I even needed to follow a traditional high school schedule. I began discussing this with friends, family, and teachers. My parents said they couldn’t help much because of their work, but they encouraged me to take responsibility for my education—managing my GPA, AP classes, dual enrollment, and CLEP exams. Yet when I talked to others, I faced a common objection: “What about your social skills? You don’t want to become like those antisocial homeschoolers, do you? And nobody’s done this before, so how do you know it will work?” I thought about those questions deeply. I responded, “Correlation doesn’t mean causation. It might not be homeschooling that makes kids antisocial, but rather that antisocial kids get homeschooled. And I plan to stay social by going to the gym with friends and attending gatherings.” But the final objection lingered: “Nobody’s done this before. How do you know it will work?” I admitted the truth: “I don’t know if it will work. But I’m willing to try, because I know I’d regret not trying, even if it ends up worse.” I remembered Jeff Bezos’s words on regret minimization: “Regrets come more from acts of omission than from acts of commission.” That was the moment I knew it was time to take the leap—time to homeschool and take control of my education in a manner I've never seen before.

For My Family, and Families Like Mine — Redesigning Wealth

Now that I had decided to homeschool, I finally had the freedom to pursue the things I loved—every single day. I created a schedule that balanced trading crypto and stocks with exploring subjects through free online platforms. For the first few months, I dove deep into everything that fascinated me, learning more each week. But when I tried to register for AP exams at my local high school, I ran into an unexpected hurdle: I missed the registration deadline. The same thing happened with dual enrollment. I had planned to take community college courses and end-of-year exams for credit, but those opportunities were no longer available. Instead of giving up, I pivoted to CLEP exams. My reasoning was simple: I could use free online resources to study for subjects that interested me and earn college credit by passing CLEP tests. But I also understood something deeper—retaining knowledge requires applying it. So that’s exactly what I did. I immersed myself in data manipulation, Python, artificial intelligence, investing, economics, financial systems, and core subjects like calculus, health, history, and writing. I built projects that included financial models, AI fine-tuning tools, and health calculators. This hands-on work transformed theory into experience—and it transformed me. Through this growth, my long-term vision crystallized: I want to create a hedge fund. Why a hedge fund? Because I saw upfront how hard it is to support a family of five on a modest income. And one day, my mom showed me her portfolio’s returns over the past ten years. They were shockingly low. Even during bull markets, her account underperformed. And in downturns, it dropped more than the S&P 500. Yet the hedge fund manager still made money—charging a 2% management fee and a 20% performance fee. It felt wrong. My parents worked so hard, only for their savings to be managed inefficiently, while the fund’s manager got paid regardless of performance. That’s when I realized: I want to build a new kind of hedge fund. One powered by AI and machine learning—designed not just to seek returns, but to serve people like my parents. This hedge fund would detect patterns in human psychology and market behavior, optimizing trades based on statistical insights. And it would have no management fees—only performance-based incentives. If the AI performs well, we succeed. If it doesn’t, we don’t get paid. That’s true alignment. Why does this matter to me? Because I’ve seen how financial systems fail everyday people. I’ve seen how hard my parents work, only to lose value in their savings over time. And I know they—like so many others—invest not just to grow wealth, but to enjoy life. To build memories with family. To feel secure. That's what I wanted my family to have. That’s what I want to help provide to others. That’s the purpose I’ve found in my journey.
